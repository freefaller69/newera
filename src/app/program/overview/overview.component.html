Two Pioneers in Down Syndrome Education mark a Fortieth Anniversary
by Rosanne Gostovich Royer

A Breakthrough in Early Intervention

In January 1971, a program was launched at the University of Washington that would gain world renown and fundamentally change the educational approach to children with Down syndrome.

Valentine Dmitriev had astonished her colleagues with the concept that intervention with children with Down syndrome should begin in infancy.

All my previous work with preschoolers and the distressing fact that there were no preschool programs for children with disabilities led me to the conclusion that training for children with Down syndrome must begin in infancy, as it does with other children, says Dmitriev.

Within a year, Patricia Oelwein joined the staff as preschool teacher of the original eleven children. She demonstrated that children with Down syndrome were not only capable readers, but were overachievers with reading scores exceeding their mental ages at all ages and reading levels.

On March 12, 2011 these two pioneers were recognized for their work at the 75th Anniversary celebration of Arc of Clark County, Washington, the very first Arc chapter in the United States. Dmitriev’s workshops in Vancouver in the 1970’s led to the establishment of PRIDE School in Vancouver, the first satellite program of the University of Washington Down Syndrome Program. PRIDE School, formerly located at Clark Community College, is now a partner in a facility with Arc of Clark County. A portion of the PRIDE school playground, the Val Dmitriev Courtyard, is dedicated to her.

How It All Began

In the late 1960’s Dmitriev put her theory into practice by implementing a pilot program and submitting a proposal to Dr. Alice Hayden, director of the Model Preschool Center at the Experimental Education Unit, University of Washington. Dr. Hayden submitted the proposal to the Handicapped Children’s Early Education Program (HCEEP) of the federal government. It was accepted and became one of 24 First Chance Programs funded through the U.S. Office of Education. When these 24 programs were evaluated in 1975, Dmitriev’s was one of only seven that the panel validated as exemplary and worthy of nationwide dissemination and replication. Eventually the concept and methodology reached six continents. In 1978 the Down Syndrome Congress recognized the program with their Meritorious Service Award, stating, "Your work has worldwide impact in proving the potential of Down's syndrome children."

When Dmitriev brought Oelwein on board, she asked her to teach a non-verbal child to read, in order to give him a means of communication. Oelwein developed a reading program based on the match, select, and name sequence that Dmitriev had taught her. In so doing she unveiled the astonishing possibility that children with Down syndrome could learn to read.

I became very excited about the effect that behavioral learning principles were having on the children, says Oelwein. They were exceeding expectations, and everything seemed possible.

These breakthroughs were made possible because of government funding that traces its beginning to 1963 when President Kennedy signed legislation to establish a national network of university based facilities for people with disabilities. The first federal legislation to provide exclusively for young children with disabilities, the Handicapped Children’s Early Education Assistance Act, was passed in 1968 as part of Lyndon Johnson’s Great Society program. Parents became students and teachers alongside their children who now had prospects for a different future.

Replication of the Program begins in Vancouver, Washington

In Vancouver, Washington, Judy and James Marick had been looking for help for their son Jason when they heard Dmitriev speak at a conference sponsored by Arc of Clark County in Vancouver in 1975. They asked her to help them set up a program for children with Down syndrome, and things happened fast.

Val invited us to her hotel room, says Marick. She set up a movie screen and slide projector and showed us what they were doing at the University of Washington. She worked with Jason on her bed in the hotel room. He was five weeks old.

Dmitriev returned to Vancouver soon after and conducted a workshop. As a result, the first University of Washington satellite program was established in 1976 in Vancouver, Washington, under the auspices of Clark Community College. Called PRIDE (Parents Receiving Individual Developmental Education), the program remained at the college for 25 years and then moved into a new facility shared with Arc of Clark County. While most programs for children with Down syndrome are now decentralized, with specialists visiting homes and then preschoolers enrolled in programs in regular school, the PRIDE constituents have kept their program as center-based as possible, following the original model.

Judy Marick is effusive about Dmitriev’s impact. My son Jason would not be where he is or who he is today, out working in the community, without Val. She gave us the tools; and it was early enough so that children who received training could go to school and the receiving teacher could have further expectations of them.

The Backgrounds of Two Remarkable Women

Valentine Dmitriev was born in Shanghai, China, of Russian parents who fled from St. Petersburg at the height of the Bolshevik revolution. From China her family emigrated to Canada, then the U.S. She graduated from the University of Washington at age nineteen with a B.A. in English and Creative Writing. As a young parent she took classes on child development and education. She worked as a nursery school teacher and then supervisor of five parent cooperative pre-schools. Her disheartening discovery of the absence of preschool programs for children with disabilities propelled her to graduate school where she earned an M.A. and began the pilot program for infants with Down syndrome, subsequently receiving a Ph.D.

Patricia Oelwein, an Arkansas native, received her degree in home economics from Henderson State University in 1960 and taught home economics four years. In the summer of 1971, she attended a workshop at the University of Washington taught by Dmitriev, and a new chapter in her life began. Excited about the impact of behavioral learning principles on the children, she applied for the soon-to-be-open teaching position in Dmitriev’s Down Syndrome Program classroom. Oelwein focused on developing curriculum and teaching methodology for preschool and kindergarten children while Dmitriev continued to focus on infants and outreach training.

Transition from Model Preschool Program to Elementary School

By the mid 1970s, when the original children in the program reached six years of age, Dr. Norris Haring, then director of the EEU, had a federally funded research program waiting for them. Accelerating and Maintaining Developmental Gains,(AMDG) with Dmitriev coordinating the project, was a collaboration with the Seattle Public Schools to continue the children’s education in an inclusive setting in regular schools. In 1981 the children entered middle school, the first children with Down syndrome to do so in Seattle.

In 1980, Dr. Rebecca Fewell became the project director of the Down Syndrome Program at the EEU. Her proposal for a project that served fathers, siblings, and grandparents (SEFAM) gave birth to the Fathers Program and the Sibling Support Project that continue today.

When federal funding for the Down Syndrome Program was no longer available in 1990, Dr. Michael Guralnick, the director of the Center on Human Development and Disability at the University of Washington, brought the program from the EEU to the center, where Oelwein provided research support, training, and counseling.

Taking the Program to Six Continents

Between the two of them, Dmitriev and Oelwein have provided training and presented at conferences throughout the USA and in 17 other countries. The first international replication of the Down Syndrome Program was established at Macquarie University in North Ryde, N.S.W., Australia in 1975, and the most recent, starting in 1991, is the Saut Education and Training Center in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, formerly known as Al-Nahda Schools for Down Syndrome. In addition, the methodology has been demonstrated in Japan, England, Russia, Indonesia, Italy, Spain, Mexico, Canada, South Korea, Venezuela, Jamaica, Guatemala, Egypt, Kuwait and Hong Kong.

The Saut Education and Training Center in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia traces its beginnings to the PRIDE School in Vancouver. A parent living in Portland, Oregon, who had heard about PRIDE School, enrolled in the program and received exceptional training for herself and her daughter. When her family moved back to Saudi Arabia she found that there were no similar programs available for children with Down syndrome. When she requested help from PRIDE School, Dmitriev and Nancy Warren, coordinator of the PRIDE program at the time, went to work immediately. They made videotapes demonstrating teaching techniques, as well as how to set up and manage preschool classrooms. All these materials were delivered via the Boeing Company’s mail service to bring the University of Washington programs to Saudi Arabia. Dmitriev’s book Time to Begin and Dmitriev & Oelwein’s Advances in Down Syndrome were the primary resources for the program.

In 1991 Oelwein became on-going consultant to the project, traveling to Saudi Arabia 23 times to develop comprehensive programs for individuals with Down syndrome, from infancy to 21 years of age. In 2010 the program received the Down Syndrome Research and Education Award for their outstanding work in the Middle East from the World Health Organization Eastern Mediterranean Region Office. The Saut Education and Training Center will be replicated in some cities, and satellite programs will be developed in other areas as needed.

Where are they now?

Forty years after her revolutionary work began, Dr. Dmitriev is in her 90s and is pursuing a distant dream from her youth writing novels. Until 2003 she continued to publish in her field and give workshops on child development education. Her books Tears and Triumphs and Time to Begin (reissued as Early Education for Children with Down Syndrome) continue to inspire readers to pursue their own education and demonstrate compellingly case by case how unique solutions can be found for each child. Kay Parks, a parent and long-time activist at PRIDE says that Dmitriev always faced tough challenges by turning to a parent and saying, Let’s figure this out.

Pat Oelwein, now in her 70s, is also retired from the University of Washington, but continues giving workshops, primarily on reading, and providing on-going support to the Saut Education and Training Center in Saudi Arabia. She is currently writing training manuals for Saut that span six programs from birth to twenty-one years of age, early intervention to community-based vocational training and supported employment. Her book, Teaching Reading to Children with Down Syndrome: A Guide for Parents and Teacher (1995), has sold over 71,000 copies. Literacy Skill Development for Students with Special Learning Needs (2007), written with Leslie Broun, expands the reading methodology to students with autism spectrum disorders and other disabilities.

Forty years ago Dmitriev sounded the cry, Don’t judge a person strictly on the basis of IQ or mental age scores but rather by what he or she is able to contribute to the human experience. Parents and others have heeded the call and made life better for many children around the world.

The Experimental Education Unit (EEU) Today

The Experimental Education Unit (EEU), where the Down Syndrome Program was launched in 1971, is currently one of three units of the Norris and Dorothy Haring Center for Applied Research and Training in Education, directed by Dr. Ilene Schwartz. The EEU is a comprehensive early childhood center providing inclusive educational services for families and children with and without disabilities from birth through kindergarten. Educational and therapeutic interventions are geared to each child’s individual needs and no child is discriminated against because of race, gender, national origin, or disability. Through coordination with local education agencies, the EEU ensures that children make a smooth transition into educational programs in their own school districts.
